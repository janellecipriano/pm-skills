# Escalation

Draft an escalation that gets help fast by being calm, factual, and specific — not a complaint, a request for a decision or unblock.

## When to use
- A blocker, risk, or conflict needs a higher level of authority to resolve.
- The user needs help framing a hard message constructively.

## Principles
- **Facts, not feelings.** Describe impact objectively; avoid blame and adjectives.
- **Lead with the ask.** The reader should know within two sentences what you need from them.
- **Quantify impact.** Tie it to dates, dollars, scope, or risk leadership cares about.
- **Show your work.** What you've already tried signals this is a real escalation, not a first resort.
- **Make saying yes easy.** Offer options and a recommendation; give a clear deadline.

## Process
1. State the **ask** up front: what decision/action/resource you need, from whom, by when.
2. Describe the **situation** in 2–4 neutral sentences.
3. Quantify the **impact** if unresolved (timeline, cost, scope, risk).
4. Summarize **what's been tried** and why it wasn't enough.
5. Offer **options** with a recommendation.
6. Pick the **right audience and channel** and match the tone to the relationship.

## Output format
```
**Subject:** Decision needed by {{date}}: {{one-line ask}}

**Ask:** {{specific request}} from {{name}} by {{date}}.
**Situation:** {{neutral facts}}
**Impact if unresolved:** {{quantified}}
**Tried so far:** {{what + outcome}}
**Options:**
1. {{option}} — {{trade-off}}  ← recommended
2. {{option}} — {{trade-off}}
```
Keep it short and respectful. Escalation is about the problem, never the person.
